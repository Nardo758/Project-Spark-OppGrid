## Feasibility Study — Industry Standards

### Required Sections

A professional feasibility study is governed by the **TELOS framework** expanded with **Management Feasibility**, yielding six core pillars[^1][^2][^3]:

1. **Market Feasibility** — Demand validation, target customer definition, competitive analysis, market trends, and traffic/demand evidence.
2. **Technical Feasibility** — Equipment, technology, production capacity, site requirements, utilities, and manufacturing/process needs.
3. **Financial Feasibility** — Startup costs, revenue projections, cash flow analysis, break-even analysis, DSCR, ROI/IRR, NPV, payback period, and sensitivity analysis.
4. **Operational Feasibility** — Organizational structure, staffing plans, supply chain, day-to-day processes, and capacity to execute.
5. **Legal/Regulatory Feasibility** — Zoning, permits, licensing, intellectual property, industry-specific regulations, and compliance requirements.
6. **Management Feasibility** — Team qualifications, industry expertise, governance structure, and succession planning (required by USDA and SBA for larger loans)[^4].

**Standard Report Structure** (bankable / lender-ready)[^1][^5][^6]:
- Title Page & Table of Contents
- Executive Summary (self-sufficient: project, key financials, risk highlights, verdict)
- Project / Business Concept Description
- Market Analysis & Demand
- Technical / Operational Requirements
- Financial Projections & Analysis (3–5 years, monthly for Year 1)
- Legal & Regulatory Considerations
- Risk Assessment Matrix (probability × impact, mitigation strategies)
- SWOT Analysis
- Conclusions & Recommendations (Go / No-Go with caveats)
- Appendices (data tables, assumption schedules, supporting sources)

### Required Data

**SBA / Lender-Specific Requirements (SOP 50 10 7.1 / 8)**[^7][^8]:
- **Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)** ≥ 1.15× on historical and/or projected cash flow; 1:1 on global basis
- **Pro-forma Balance Sheet** adjusted for loan proceeds, equity injection, and use of funds
- **Ratio benchmarks**: Current Ratio, Debt/Tangible Net Worth, plus industry-specific ratios (inventory turnover, receivables turnover)
- **Working capital adequacy** analysis for minimum 12 months post-close
- **Collateral adequacy** assessment
- **Insurance requirements**: life insurance on key principals, hazard & liability
- **Revenue growth justification**: new products, channels, facilities; comparison to current industry trends
- **Equity injection**: 10% minimum for startups / change-of-ownership under SOP 50 10 8
- **Independent third-party preparation** — objectivity is not optional for SBA compliance[^9]

**Credible Data Points Expected by Institutional Reviewers**[^5][^6][^10]:
- Market demand backed by traffic counts, consumer surveys, or demand signals (not just opinion)
- Competitor market share and pricing, not just a count of nearby businesses
- Operating cost benchmarks tied to actual labor/material rates (BLS QCEW, local wage data)
- Real estate / lease comparables for site-based businesses
- Seasonality and ramp-up curves (e.g., hospitality: 40–55% Year 1 occupancy, 65–80% stabilization over 18–36 months)
- Break-even analysis with unit-level economics
- Sensitivity tables on the 4–6 variables that materially impact IRR / cash flow
- Risk register with clear mitigation plans, not generic lists

## Market Analysis — Industry Standards

### Required Sections

A professional market analysis report (investor-grade / consultant-grade) typically contains[^11][^12][^13]:

1. **Executive Summary** — Key findings, market size, growth rate, competitive verdict, and top 3 opportunities.
2. **Industry Overview & Trends** — Industry definition, NAICS classification, lifecycle stage, key growth drivers, regulatory changes, and macro trends (interest rates, inflation, consumer confidence).
3. **Market Size (TAM / SAM / SOM)** — Hierarchical sizing with dual-methodology validation.
4. **Competitive Landscape** — Market share mapping, competitor profiles, strategic group analysis, Porter's Five Forces, and barrier-to-entry assessment.
5. **Consumer / Customer Analysis** — Target demographics, psychographics, buying behavior, price sensitivity, and unmet needs.
6. **Channel & Distribution Analysis** — How products/services reach the customer; online vs. offline, D2C vs. wholesale.
7. **Growth Opportunities & White Space** — Underserved segments, geographic gaps, emerging demand signals.
8. **Risk Factors & Market Constraints** — Saturation risk, regulatory headwinds, supply chain vulnerabilities, substitution threats.
9. **Data Sources & Methodology** — Transparency on how every number was derived.
10. **Appendices** — Raw data tables, competitor financials, survey instruments.

### TAM/SAM/SOM Methodology

Investors and lenders expect **dual-methodology cross-validation**[^11][^14][^15]:

**Top-Down Approach** (best for TAM):
- Start with industry-wide figures from analyst reports (IBISWorld, Gartner, Statista, Euromonitor).
- Apply geographic and segment filters to narrow to SAM.
- Pros: Quick, uses recognized sources.
- Cons: Can be misleading if not validated locally; may overlook niche dynamics.

**Bottom-Up Approach** (best for SAM/SOM; preferred by investors):
- Build from unit economics: `(Number of Target Customers × Annual Revenue per Customer)`.
- For SOM: apply realistic market-share capture (1–5% for early-stage entrants; 5–15% for established players; 15–30% for market leaders)[^15].
- Base SOM on sales capacity, channel reach, and conversion rates, not aspirational targets.
- Pros: Grounded in actual business model; more credible.
- Cons: Data-intensive; requires accurate customer counts and pricing.

**Value-Theory Approach** (for disruptive/new categories):
- Estimate the economic value created for customers vs. alternatives.
- Determine willingness-to-pay percentage.
- Useful when benchmarks are limited.

**Best Practice**: Present **both top-down and bottom-up** side-by-side. If they converge, it validates assumptions. If they diverge, investigate before pitching[^15].

**SOM Credibility Rules**:
- Early-stage startups: 1–5% of SAM is realistic.
- Claiming 10%+ market share in Year 1 without a proven moat destroys credibility.
- Show the math: `X customers × $Y price × Z% conversion = SOM`.

### Competitive Landscape Standards

**Expected Frameworks**[^16][^17]:
- **Porter's Five Forces** — Threat of new entrants, bargaining power of suppliers, bargaining power of buyers, threat of substitutes, competitive rivalry.
- **Strategic Group Mapping** — Plot competitors on axes (e.g., price vs. quality; geographic coverage vs. service breadth) to reveal positioning gaps.
- **Competitive Set / Peer Benchmarking** — For site-based businesses, identify 3–5 direct competitors; compare on ADR, occupancy, revenue per available unit, ratings, and review sentiment.
- **Market Share Estimation** — Use BLS establishment counts + IBISWorld revenue data to estimate local competitor revenue (not just star ratings).
- **Barrier-to-Entry Assessment** — Capital requirements, regulatory hurdles, brand loyalty, network effects.
