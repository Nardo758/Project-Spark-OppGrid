## Part 1: PESTLE Analysis Research

### 1.1 Framework Standards

PESTLE (Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental) is a structured macro-environmental scanning framework used for strategic planning, market entry, and investment decisions. Academic literature confirms it is among the most widely adopted external environment analysis tools in management and consulting [^1][^2].

Key standards across sources:
- **Each factor must include specific, measurable indicators** rather than narrative descriptions [^3].
- **Data-driven weighting** is recommended — PESTLE should not be purely qualitative; quantitative indicators should be scored and weighted [^4].
- **Interconnectedness** between factors should be explicitly analyzed (e.g., how political trade policy affects economic exchange rates) [^5].
- **Time horizon** matters — PESTLE should distinguish between short-term (<2 years), medium-term (2–5 years), and long-term (>5 years) impacts [^1].
- **Industry specificity** — generic PESTLE is considered low-value; each factor must be adapted to the specific industry (e.g., restaurants vs. tech vs. retail) [^6][^7].

### 1.4 How PESTLE is Used in Consulting (McKinsey, Deloitte, PwC Methodology)

Consulting firms apply PESTLE with additional rigor:

1. **Structured Scoring Matrices:** Each factor is scored on impact (1–5) and probability (1–5), then plotted on a risk-opportunity matrix. This is derived from risk management frameworks commonly used in strategy consulting [^11].
2. **Scenario Planning:** PESTLE factors are combined into 3–4 scenarios (optimistic, baseline, pessimistic, disruptive) for stress-testing strategy [^11].
3. **Industry Benchmarking:** PESTLE is compared against industry-specific frameworks (e.g., for healthcare, regulatory is weighted higher; for tech, technological is weighted higher) [^11].
4. **Weighted Factor Analysis:** Not all PESTLE factors are equal. Consulting firms apply Analytical Hierarchy Process (AHP) or similar to weight factors by industry relevance [^4].
5. **Cross-Impact Analysis:** Systematic mapping of how changes in one factor cascade to others (e.g., a new environmental regulation → increased compliance costs → higher prices → reduced consumer demand) [^5].
6. **Actionable Output:** PESTLE is not just descriptive; it must produce a prioritized action list with owners, timelines, and KPIs. This is the standard in consulting deliverables [^11].

## Part 2: Location Analysis Research

### 2.1 Site Selection Methodology (CoStar / REIS / ESRI Standards)

Professional site selection follows a multi-layered GIS and data-driven methodology. Academic literature and industry standards identify the following core components [^16][^17][^18]:

1. **Market Demand Analysis** — TAM/SAM/SOM calculation based on population, income, and spending patterns within the trade area
2. **Trade Area Delineation** — Not just radius; based on drive time, pedestrian flow, or public transit accessibility
3. **Competitive Landscape Mapping** — GIS-based competitor placement with market share estimation
4. **Demographic & Psychographic Profiling** — Detailed customer profile matching target market to local population
5. **Site Characteristics Assessment** — Physical attributes, visibility, access, parking, co-tenancy
6. **Traffic & Accessibility Analysis** — Vehicle counts, pedestrian flow, transit proximity
7. **Economic & Cost Analysis** — Lease rates, operating costs, labor costs, tax burden
8. **Regulatory & Zoning Review** — Land use compatibility, permitting requirements, signage restrictions
9. **Supply Chain & Logistics Proximity** — For businesses with physical inventory or distribution needs
10. **Incentive & Tax Credit Analysis** — Local, state, and federal programs that reduce net cost

### 2.2 Demographic Analysis Standards

Professional demographic analysis for site selection goes beyond basic census data [^16][^19]:

| Indicator | Standard Methodology | Data Source |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------|
| **Daytime Population** | Residents + inbound commuters – outbound commuters; critical for businesses serving daytime crowds (lunch, retail, services) | ACS + LEHD Origin-Destination Employment Statistics (LODES) | 
| **Workforce Availability** | Working-age population (18–64), labor force participation rate, unemployment rate, educational attainment by field | BLS QCEW, Census ACS, O*NET |
| **Education Levels** | % with Bachelor's+, % with high school, vocational training availability | Census ACS, NCES, state workforce boards |
| **Household Income Distribution** | Not just median — quintile breakdown to identify target market concentration | Census ACS |
| **Spending Potential Index (SPI)** | Measures relative spending power compared to U.S. average | ESRI Business Analyst, Nielsen PRIZM |
| **Population Growth Trajectory** | 5-year and 10-year projected growth rates | Census projections, Woods & Poole, state demographers |
| **Age Cohort Analysis** | Detailed age pyramids; critical for businesses targeting specific life stages | Census ACS |
| **Psychographic Segmentation** | Lifestyle clusters (e.g., "Urban Achievers," "Family Focused") | ESRI Tapestry, Nielsen PRIZM, Claritas |

### 2.3 Traffic Analysis Standards

Traffic is one of the most validated predictors of retail site success [^17][^20]:

| Measure | Standard | Application |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Average Annual Daily Traffic (AADT)** | Vehicle counts per day from state DOTs | Assesses raw exposure potential |
| **Peak Hour Traffic** | AM (7–9 AM) and PM (4–6 PM) counts | Critical for restaurants, coffee shops, gas stations |
| **Traffic Growth Trend** | 3-year historical trend + projected growth | Validates future viability |
| **Pedestrian Flow Counts** | Foot traffic sensors, mobile device data, manual counts | Essential for urban retail, restaurants in walkable areas |
| **Public Transit Accessibility** | Bus/rail stop proximity, frequency, ridership | Critical for urban locations; walk score integration |
| **Ingress / Egress Quality** | Number of curb cuts, turn restrictions, visibility from road | Affects customer convenience and impulse visits |
| **Parking Availability** | Spaces per 1,000 sq ft, shared parking ratios | Critical for suburban retail and restaurants |
| **Walk Score / Bike Score** | Composite indices of pedestrian/bicycle accessibility | Correlates with foot traffic in urban markets |

### 2.4 Lease Rate Comparables Methodology

Commercial lease analysis follows established appraisal and brokerage standards [^21][^22]:

1. **Comparable Property Selection:**
   - Same property type (retail, office, industrial, restaurant)
   - Similar size (±20% of target square footage)
   - Similar location quality (same submarket or trade area)
   - Recent transactions (within 6–12 months)
   - Similar condition and build-out quality

2. **Key Metrics Calculated:**
   - **Effective Rent:** Base rent minus concessions (free rent, TI allowances)
   - **Rent per Square Foot (PSF):** Annual or monthly, NNN or gross
   - **Percentage Rent:** For retail — rent based on % of gross sales above breakpoint
   - **Tenant Improvement (TI) Allowance:** $/sq ft provided by landlord
   - **Lease Term & Escalation:** Annual rent bumps, CPI adjustments
   - **Occupancy Cost Ratio:** Total rent + CAM + utilities as % of projected revenue

3. **Data Sources:**
   - CoStar, REIS, LoopNet (commercial listing databases)
   - Local broker market reports (CBRE, JLL, Colliers, Cushman & Wakefield)
   - Municipal property tax records
   - SEC filings (for public REITs with disclosure requirements)

### 2.5 Zoning and Regulatory Analysis

Zoning analysis is a critical but often overlooked component of site selection [^23][^24]:

| Element | What to Analyze | Data Source |
|---------|----------------|-------------|
| **Zoning Classification** | Is the proposed use permitted by right, conditional, or prohibited? | Municipal zoning maps, planning department |
| **Use Permissions** | Specific use categories (e.g., restaurant vs. fast food vs. bar) | Zoning ordinance text |
| **Development Standards** | Lot coverage, setbacks, height limits, FAR (floor area ratio) | Zoning ordinance, subdivision regulations |
| **Parking Requirements** | Minimum spaces required by zoning code | Zoning ordinance parking chapter |
| **Signage Regulations** | Size, height, illumination, digital sign restrictions | Sign ordinance |
| **Historic District / Overlay** | Special design review, restrictions on exterior changes | Historic preservation commission |
| **Health & Safety Permits** | Food service permits, fire safety, occupancy limits | County health department, fire marshal |
| **Liquor Licensing** | ABC license availability, proximity restrictions (distance from schools, churches) | State ABC board, local licensing |
| **Environmental Restrictions** | Flood zones, wetlands, contaminated sites (brownfields) | FEMA flood maps, EPA CERCLIS, state environmental agencies |
| **Planned Developments** | Upcoming road construction, zoning changes, redevelopment plans | Municipal comprehensive plans, capital improvement plans |

### 2.6 Competition Mapping Standards

Professional competition analysis follows GIS-based spatial modeling standards [^25][^26]:

1. **Radius / Drive-Time Analysis:**
   - **Convenience retail:** 1–3 mile radius
   - **Restaurant / casual dining:** 3–5 mile radius
   - **Specialty retail:** 5–10 mile radius
   - **Big-box / destination:** 10–20 mile radius
   - **Drive-time is preferred over Euclidean distance** in suburban / auto-dependent markets [^25]

2. **Market Share Estimation:**
   - **Huff Gravity Model:** Probability of consumer patronizing a store = function of store size/attractiveness ÷ distance^λ
   - **Reilly's Law of Retail Gravitation:** Trade area boundaries based on relative size and distance of competing centers
   - **Spatial Interaction Models:** Estimate sales cannibalization and market share capture [^26]

3. **Competitor Attribute Profiling:**
   - Square footage, seating capacity, hours, price positioning
   - Online ratings, review sentiment, review volume (recent velocity)
   - Years in business (experience proxy), chain vs. independent
   - Social media following, engagement rates
   - Estimated revenue (using industry benchmarks × known metrics)

4. **Gap Analysis:**
   - **Category gap:** Underserved product/service categories
   - **Geographic gap:** Underserved areas within the trade area
   - **Temporal gap:** Competitors closed during high-demand hours (e.g., late night, early morning) [^25]

### 2.7 Supply Chain Proximity Analysis

For retail, manufacturing, and logistics businesses, supply chain proximity is critical [^27][^28]:

| Factor | Measurement | Data Source |
|--------|------------|-------------|
| **Distribution center proximity** | Miles to nearest DC, delivery frequency | Company data, logistics provider networks |
| **Transportation infrastructure** | Highway access (interstate proximity), rail, port, airport | DOT, BTS freight data |
| **Last-mile delivery cost** | Cost per delivery by zone | UPS/FedEx zone maps, carrier rate cards |
| **Supplier density** | Number of relevant suppliers within radius | Industry directories, supplier databases |
| **Labor cost for logistics** | Warehouse worker wages, driver wages | BLS OES, state workforce data |
| **Inventory carrying cost** | Rent per sq ft for warehouse + insurance + taxes | CoStar, CBRE market reports |
| **Proximity to customers** | Average shipping distance to end customers | Customer address data, geocoding |

### 2.8 Incentive and Tax Credit Analysis

Professional site selection includes comprehensive incentive analysis [^29][^30]:

| Incentive Type | Examples | Data Source |
|---------------|----------|-------------|
| **Federal** | New Markets Tax Credit (NMTC), Opportunity Zones, Historic Tax Credits, Energy Tax Credits (ITC/PTC), Work Opportunity Tax Credit | CDFI Fund, IRS, Treasury |
| **State** | Job creation tax credits, investment tax credits, R&D tax credits, sales tax exemptions, property tax abatements | State economic development agencies, state tax departments |
| **Local** | TIF (Tax Increment Financing), PILOT (Payment in Lieu of Taxes), free land, infrastructure grants, expedited permitting | City/m county economic development offices, redevelopment authorities |
| **Utility** | Rate discounts, infrastructure extensions, renewable energy rebates | Local utility companies, PUCs |
| **Special Districts** | Enterprise Zones, Foreign Trade Zones, Industrial Development Bonds | State economic development agencies |

**Key Analytical Standards:**
- Net present value (NPV) of incentive package vs. baseline
- Compliance requirements (job creation thresholds, wage floors, clawback provisions)
- Time-to-receipt (some incentives take 2–3 years to materialize)
- Stackability (can multiple incentives be combined?)
