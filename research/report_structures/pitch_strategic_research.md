## Part 1: Pitch Deck Research — What Investors Expect

### 1.1 The Dominant Framework: Sequoia Capital

Sequoia Capital's pitch deck template, first published in 2015 and refined since, is widely considered the **unofficial global standard** for early-stage investor decks[^1][^2]. It is the framework that powered investments in Apple, Google, Airbnb, Stripe, and WhatsApp.

**Sequoia's Core 10–12 Slide Structure:**

| # | Slide | Purpose |
|---|-------|---------|
| 1 | **Title / Company Purpose** | One-line identity: Who are you and what are you building? |
| 2 | **Problem** | What is broken in the world? Why is it urgent? |
| 3 | **Solution** | How do you fix it? What is the unique insight? |
| 4 | **Why Now?** | What recent change makes this business viable *today*? |
| 5 | **Market Size** | TAM / SAM / SOM with defensible methodology |
| 6 | **Competition** | Competitive landscape + your differentiation |
| 7 | **Product** | What does the product actually do? Demo if possible |
| 8 | **Business Model** | How you make money: pricing, revenue per customer, unit economics |
| 9 | **Team** | Why are YOU the right people to build this? |
| 10 | **Financials** | Revenue trajectory, projections, key assumptions |
| 11 | **The Ask** | How much, what it's for, expected outcome |
| 12 | **Appendix** | Detailed metrics, technical depth, extended financials |

**Key Principle:** The deck is built around **investor risk evaluation**, not founder enthusiasm. Every slide must answer one question: *"Why should I invest?"*[^1]. Investors spend under **3 minutes** scanning decks in 2025; clarity and narrative flow are paramount[^3].

### 1.2 Y Combinator Pitch Deck Standards

Y Combinator provides a **seed-stage-specific template** that emphasizes traction, clarity, and founder-market fit[^4][^5].

**YC Core Components:**
- **Problem** — Identify the problem your product aims to solve
- **Solution** — Showcase how your offering addresses the problem
- **Product** — Clear description + demo if available
- **Traction** — Metrics that indicate growth potential (MRR, customers, growth rate)
- **Market Size** — Quantified opportunity
- **Business Model** — Revenue generation mechanism
- **Team** — Qualifications and domain expertise
- **Financials** — Current situation + future projections

**YC Philosophy (Paul Graham):** *"A pitch deck should answer two questions: What are you going to do with the money, and what's the big vision?"*[^5]. YC decks are intentionally **boring, simple, and functional** — not design projects, but sales documents[^4].

### 1.3 Andreessen Horowitz (a16z) Standards

a16z evaluates decks with a focus on **structured thinking, unit economics, and team superpowers**[^6][^7].

**a16z Slide-by-Slide Expectations:**

| Slide | a16z Priority |
|-------|---------------|
| Opening | Identity without noise: one line on who you are and what you build |
| Problem | Business inefficiency, workflow mess, or market gap — logical, not emotional |
| Business Model | Pricing, average revenue per customer, scalability — *"Don't say 'we'll figure this out later'"* |
| Traction | Revenue, growth rate, retention, engagement — signal over noise |
| Go-To-Market | Specific wedge, not buzzwords (SEO, content, influencers). How will you *win* customers? |
| Team | Why YOU? Domain expertise, lived the problem, distribution advantage |
| Roadmap | MVP → V1 → V2+ — shows strategic direction, not just features |
| Ask | Direct: *"We're raising $X to do Y over Z months"* |

**a16z Investment Criteria:**
1. **Founder "superpower"** — unique insight or skill[^7]
2. **Massive market** — must be large enough for billion-dollar outcomes
3. **Unique technological advantage / moat** — defensible, hard to replicate
4. **Operational support readiness** — a16z provides 150+ person operating platform

### 1.4 Mandatory Slides & Data Requirements

Across Sequoia, YC, and a16z, the following slides are **non-negotiable**[^1][^3][^8]:

| Slide | Required Data | Benchmarks / Standards |
|-------|---------------|------------------------|
| **Problem** | Market pain quantified (time lost, money wasted, customers affected) | One great stat > five forgettable ones |
| **Solution** | Unique insight, demo video (60–90 sec if available) | Must be defensible and differentiated |
| **Market Size** | TAM / SAM / SOM with bottom-up or top-down methodology | Investors need $1B+ TAM for venture returns |
| **Business Model** | Pricing, revenue streams, unit economics | Clear path to profitability |
| **Traction** | Stage-appropriate metrics (see table below) | Growth trajectory > absolute numbers |
| **Team** | Faces, names, roles, one-liner relevance | Previous successes, domain expertise |
| **Competition** | Competitive matrix, differentiation | Acknowledge reality; don't claim "no competition" |
| **Financials** | 12–36 month projections, key assumptions | Conservative > optimistic |
| **Ask** | Round size, use of funds, milestones | Confident and specific |

**Stage-Appropriate Traction Metrics:**[^8][^9]

| Stage | Key Metrics | Good Benchmarks |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Pre-Seed** | Waitlist, LOIs, pilot customers, DAU/MAU | 1,000+ waitlist, 5–10 LOIs, 50%+ DAU/MAU |
| **Seed** | MRR, MoM growth, paying customers, churn | $10–50K MRR, 15–25% MoM growth, <5% monthly churn |
| **Series A** | ARR, YoY growth, NRR, LTV:CAC, payback | $1–3M+ ARR, 100%+ YoY, 100–130%+ NRR, 3:1+ LTV:CAC |
| **Series B+** | ARR, magic number, NRR, market share | $8–15M+ ARR, 80–100%+ YoY, 0.75+ magic number |

### 1.5 Unit Economics & Financial Data Requirements

Investors scrutinize **unit economics** as the primary indicator of scalability[^9][^10].

**The Four Metrics Investors Care About Most:**

| Metric | Formula | Benchmark |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **CAC** | Total S&M spend / New customers acquired | Declining over time |
| **LTV** | ARPU x Gross Margin / Churn Rate | Growing over time |
| **LTV:CAC Ratio** | LTV / CAC | **3:1 or higher** (gold standard for Series A) |
| **Payback Period** | CAC / (Monthly Revenue per Customer x Gross Margin %) | **<12 months** (SMB SaaS), **<18 months** (Enterprise) |

**Additional Metrics by Model:**[^9][^10]
- **SaaS:** ARR, MRR, Net Revenue Retention (NRR), Gross Margin (70–85% healthy), Churn (monthly or annual), Magic Number (>0.75 = efficient growth)
- **Transactional:** Revenue per transaction, repeat rate, margin
- **Marketplace:** GMV, take rate, network effects, liquidity metrics

### 1.6 Fundable vs. Unfundable Pitch Decks

**Fundable Decks:**
- Follow a known structure (Sequoia/YC) so investors can pattern-match efficiently[^1]
- Lead with the **strongest signal** (traction, market size, or team)[^3]
- Include a **"Why Now?"** slide — only 46% of decks do, but it's a major differentiator[^3]
- Show **improving metrics over time** (declining CAC, increasing LTV, improving retention)[^9]
- Use **30-point font minimum** (Guy Kawasaki's 10/20/30 rule) — forces clarity and prevents hiding weak arguments in paragraphs[^1]
- Keep main flow to **10–15 slides**; every slide past 15 weakens the pitch[^1]

**Unfundable Decks:**
- **47+ slides** with molecular structures on slide 1 (real example: biotech founder rejected 23 times, raised £3.2M after rebuilding to 10 slides)[^11]
- Missing **Problem** or **Solution** clarity
- Unsupported financial projections (no methodology, no assumptions)
- Generic market sizing (top-down only, no bottom-up validation)
- Cluttered traction slides with vanity metrics (page views, likes, signups without engagement)
- **"We'll figure out monetization later"** — immediate rejection signal[^6]
- AI-generated generic narratives that sound identical to hundreds of other decks[^12]

## Part 2: Strategic Assessment Research — What Professional Consulting Deliverables Contain

### 2.1 McKinsey / BCG / Bain Strategic Assessment Frameworks

Top-tier consulting firms (McKinsey, BCG, Bain) follow a **structured, phased methodology** for strategic assessments[^13][^14].

**Common 5-Phase Consulting Methodology:**

| Phase | Activities | Deliverables |
|-------|------------|--------------|
| 1. **Discovery & Assessment** | Stakeholder interviews, market analysis, competitive benchmarking, regulatory review | Assessment Report, Benchmarking Analysis |
| 2. **Strategy Formulation** | Scenario planning, visioning workshops, strategic option development, risk assessment | Strategic Roadmap, Business Case |
| 3. **Validation** | Stress-test against market scenarios, financial modeling, sensitivity analysis | Risk Assessment Report, Financial Model |
| 4. **Execution Planning** | Project charters, governance structures, resource allocation, change management | Implementation Plan, Change Management Framework |
| 5. **Monitoring & Adaptation** | KPI dashboards, performance reviews, continuous improvement | Performance Reports, Optimization Plan |

**Key Principles:**
- **Hypothesis-driven:** Start with a testable hypothesis, then validate or refine with data[^15]
- **MECE structuring:** Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive — no overlaps, no gaps
- **Data triangulation:** Every critical finding backed by 2+ independent sources[^15]
- **Executive-ready deliverables:** PowerPoint, Excel models, Word reports — all client-facing quality[^13]

### 2.2 SWOT Analysis Standards & Best Practices

SWOT is a foundational framework but must be applied **rigorously** to be valuable in a consulting context[^16].

**Professional SWOT Standards:**

| Element | Requirements |
|---------|--------------|
| **Strengths** | Internal capabilities that create competitive advantage; must be **specific** and **defensible** |
| **Weaknesses** | Internal gaps that expose the business to risk; must be **honest** (not aspirational) |
| **Opportunities** | External market conditions that the business can exploit; linked to **actionable strategies** |
| **Threats** | External forces that could harm the business; must include **probability/impact** assessment |

**Best Practices:**
- Link each SWOT element to **specific data points** (not generic statements)
- Prioritize by **impact x probability** (not just listing factors)
- Derive **TOWS Matrix** (strategic actions from SWOT intersections) — e.g., use Strengths to exploit Opportunities (S-O strategies)
- Refresh quarterly; static SWOTs become stale quickly

### 2.3 Porter's Five Forces Implementation Requirements

Porter's Five Forces is the standard for **industry structure analysis** and competitive intensity[^15].

**The Five Forces:**

| Force | Key Questions | Data Requirements |
|-------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Threat of New Entrants** | Barriers to entry: capital, regulation, technology, brand loyalty | Industry concentration ratios, startup failure rates, capital requirements |
| **Bargaining Power of Suppliers** | Supplier concentration, switching costs, uniqueness of inputs | Supplier market share, number of suppliers, input price volatility |
| **Bargaining Power of Buyers** | Buyer concentration, price sensitivity, switching costs | Customer concentration, price elasticity, churn rates |
| **Threat of Substitutes** | Availability of alternatives, price/performance trade-offs | Cross-price elasticity, technology disruption trends |
| **Competitive Rivalry** | Number of competitors, industry growth, exit barriers | Market share data, HHI index, revenue growth rates, margin compression |

**Implementation Requirements:**
- Must include **quantified scoring** (1–5 scale) for each force, not just narrative
- Requires **industry-specific data** — generic analysis is useless
- Should conclude with **strategic implications**: which forces are strongest and how to mitigate/ leverage them

### 2.4 Ansoff Matrix for Growth Strategy

The Ansoff Matrix (Product/Market Expansion Grid) evaluates **growth options by risk**[^17][^18].

| | **Existing Products** | **New Products** |
|---|---|---|
| **Existing Markets** | **Market Penetration** (lowest risk) | **Product Development** (moderate risk) |
| **New Markets** | **Market Development** (moderate risk) | **Diversification** (highest risk) |

**Implementation Steps:**
1. **Assess current position** — revenue growth, churn, CAC, LTV, market position
2. **Map opportunities** — 2–3 ideas per quadrant
3. **Evaluate risk vs. resources** — score each on risk, resource needs, time to impact, strategic fit
4. **Prioritize 1–2 strategies** — most companies should focus on one primary + one secondary
5. **Build execution roadmap** — 30/60/90-day milestones, DRIs, budgets
6. **Track & adjust** — weekly leading indicators, monthly KPIs, quarterly reassessment[^17]

### 2.5 Strategic Positioning Frameworks

#### Blue Ocean Strategy
Blue Ocean Strategy (Kim & Mauborgne) focuses on **creating uncontested market space** rather than competing in existing markets[^19][^20].

**Core Tools:**
- **Strategy Canvas:** Visual map of competitive factors — reveals where everyone converges (red ocean) and where differentiation is possible
- **Four Actions Framework (ERRC):**
  - **Eliminate:** Which industry factors can be removed entirely?
  - **Reduce:** Which factors can be reduced below industry standard?
  - **Raise:** Which factors should be raised well above industry norms?
  - **Create:** Which new factors should the industry offer for the first time?
- **Value Innovation:** Simultaneously pursue differentiation AND low cost — breaking the traditional trade-off[^19]

#### Value Disciplines (Treacy & Wiersema)
Three paths to competitive advantage:
1. **Operational Excellence** — Best total cost and convenience (e.g., Walmart, Amazon)
2. **Product Leadership** — Best product, continuous innovation (e.g., Apple, Tesla)
3. **Customer Intimacy** — Best solution for each customer, deep relationships (e.g., Nordstrom, Salesforce)

**Requirement:** A company must **lead** in one discipline while maintaining **threshold standards** in the other two. Trying to lead in all three leads to mediocrity.

### 2.6 Competitive Advantage Analysis Methodology

Professional competitive analysis requires **structured comparison**, not just listing competitors[^15][^16].

**Required Components:**

| Component | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Competitor Benchmarking Table** | Side-by-side comparison on price, features, quality, service, market share, growth rate |
| **Competitive Positioning Map** | 2x2 matrix (e.g., Price vs. Quality) showing where each player sits |
| **Market Share Analysis** | Revenue or volume share, trend over time, concentration ratio (HHI) |
| **Differentiation Factors** | What makes each competitor unique — sustainable advantages vs. temporary tactics |
| **Vulnerability Assessment** | Competitor weaknesses that can be exploited (pricing, service gaps, customer churn) |
| **Whitespace Analysis** | Underserved segments or unmet needs where no competitor is strong |

### 2.7 Data Sources Used in Strategic Consulting Deliverables

Top consulting firms use a **hierarchy of data sources** with triangulation[^15][^21].

**Tier 1 Sources (Highest Credibility):**
- Primary research: customer surveys, stakeholder interviews, expert network calls
- Proprietary firm databases: McKinsey Global Institute, BCG Henderson Institute
- Government statistical agencies: U.S. Census, BLS, BEA, FRED
- Regulatory filings: SEC 10-K, 10-Q, S-1, industry-specific filings

**Tier 2 Sources:**
- Industry analyst reports: Gartner, Forrester, IDC, IBISWorld
- Trade associations and industry bodies
- Academic research and peer-reviewed studies
- Financial databases: Bloomberg, Capital IQ, PitchBook

**Tier 3 Sources (Used with Caution):**
- News articles and press releases
- Company websites and marketing materials
- Social media and review aggregators (for sentiment, not hard data)

**Consulting Deliverable Standards:**
- Every insight must be **backed by solid numbers and cited sources**[^15]
- **Multi-source triangulation** for critical findings (2+ independent sources confirming the same data point)
- **Sensitivity analysis** on all projections and market size estimates
- **Assumption documentation** — every number must have its assumptions visible
